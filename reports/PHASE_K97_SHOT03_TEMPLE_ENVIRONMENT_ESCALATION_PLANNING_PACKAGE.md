## Environmental Combat Design

The temple should function as a martial-arts tool, not only as a backdrop.

Important interaction zones:

- corridors
- stairs
- pillar lines
- eaves
- rooftop edge
- roof tiles
- rain-slick stone floor
- wooden railings
- hanging lanterns, prayer banners, or similar foreground occluders if present in the selected reference

Good SHOT-03 action should show characters using architectural geometry: being forced along a corridor line, rebounding from a railing, pivoting around a pillar, losing traction on wet stairs, sliding on roof tiles, or recovering balance near an eave.

Do not escalate into full building collapse yet. SHOT-04 should keep that territory.

## Action Design Requirements

The action language should stay physical and readable.

Required body-mechanics emphasis:

- foot traction
- rain-slick slips and recovery steps
- pivots around stairs, pillars, or railing edges
- shoulder turns and hip changes during deflection
- hard contact points with rebound
- defender reactions through torso displacement, guard collapse, forced sidestep, or foot skid

Avoid:

- soft push-hands rhythm
- repeated gentle forearm checks
- long held binds
- weak contact without recoil
- static posing while the camera moves
- particles or rain effects hiding the contact

SHOT-03 should preserve the lesson from SHOT-02: strong clips need clear collision, weight transfer, foot feedback, and contact readability.

## Camera and Spatial Design

Spatial clarity is critical because architecture can easily confuse staging.

Camera requirements:

- Use architectural anchors: pillars, roofline, stair edge, eaves, corridor opening, railing line.
- Keep foreground/background roles clear.
- Keep Fenshou and Shuangji readable as two separate fighters.
- Track movement laterally through a corridor or pillar line when needed.
- Rise only if rooftop or stair movement is justified by footwork.
- Avoid excessive shake that hides contact.
- Avoid losing the fighters behind columns, rain, or eaves for too long.

The camera can move, but the fighters must keep moving too. Do not repeat the old failure mode where the camera pulls back while both characters remain frozen.

## Candidate Route Options

### Route A - Column / Corridor To Roof Edge

Description: the fight moves through a pillar or corridor line and ends near eaves or a roof edge.

Benefits:

- Strong continuity from SHOT-02 floor-level combat.
- Pillars and corridor geometry give clean staging anchors.
- The environment can be used tactically without premature destruction.
- Good place for shoulder checks, forearm deflections, foot skids, and railing rebound.

Risks:

- Columns may hide contact if the camera drifts too much.
- The model may treat the corridor and pillars as passive background unless the prompt demands physical interaction.

### Route B - Wet Stairs To Rooftop

Description: the fight climbs wet stairs and transitions onto roof tiles.

Benefits:

- Clear escalation from floor combat into vertical temple architecture.
- Wet stairs and roof tiles naturally support slip, pivot, slide, and recovery.
- Sets up later SHOT-04 roof/structure damage without destroying the temple in SHOT-03.

Risks:

- Rooftop movement may become floaty if foot contact is not tightly anchored.
- A sudden move from courtyard to rooftop may feel discontinuous if the selected start frame does not bridge the level change.

### Route C - Roof-Only Backup

Description: the shot starts on roof tiles and uses sliding, eaves, and roof edge movement.

Benefits:

- Strong roof silhouette and tile-slide possibilities.
- Useful if the human wants SHOT-03 to immediately declare a new action plane.

Risks:

- May lose continuity from SHOT-02 if there is no bridge from the courtyard fight.
- May prematurely feel like SHOT-04/05 if roof damage or collapse becomes too large.

### Recommended Route For K98

If the human wants a default, Route A/B hybrid is the cleanest next choice:

Start with corridor or pillar-line pressure, then angle into wet stairs or eaves movement. This preserves SHOT-02 continuity while making SHOT-03 clearly about architecture.

Human route decision is still preferred before K98 creates a prompt package.
